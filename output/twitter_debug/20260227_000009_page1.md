'Anthropic understands that the Department of War, not private companies, makes military decisions. We have never raised objections to particular military operations nor attempted to limit use of our technology in an ad hoc manner.

However, in a narrow set of cases, we believe AI can undermine, rather than defend, democratic values. Some uses are also simply outside the bounds of what today’s technology can safely and reliably do. Two such use cases have never been included in our contracts with the Department of War, and we believe they should not be included now:

\- Mass domestic surveillance. We support the use of AI for lawful foreign intelligence and counterintelligence missions. But using these systems for mass domestic surveillance is incompatible with democratic values. AI-driven mass surveillance presents serious, novel risks to our fundamental liberties. To the extent that such surveillance is currently legal, this is only because the law has not yet caught up with the rapidly growing capabilities of AI. For example, under current law, the government can purchase detailed records of Americans’ movements, web browsing, and associations from public sources without obtaining a warrant, a practice the Intelligence Community has acknowledged raises privacy concerns and that has generated bipartisan opposition in Congress. Powerful AI makes it possible to assemble this scattered, individually innocuous data into a comprehensive picture of any person’s life—automatically and at massive scale.

\- Fully autonomous weapons. Partially autonomous weapons, like those used today in Ukraine, are vital to the defense of democracy. Even fully autonomous weapons (those that take humans out of the loop entirely and automate selecting and engaging targets) may prove critical for our national defense. But today, frontier AI systems are simply not reliable enough to power fully autonomous weapons. We will not knowingly provide a product that puts America’s warfighters and civilians at risk. We have offered to work directly with the Department of War on R&D to improve the reliability of these systems, but they have not accepted this offer. In addition, without proper oversight, fully autonomous weapons cannot be relied upon to exercise the critical judgment that our highly trained, professional troops exhibit every day. They need to be deployed with proper guardrails, which don’t exist today.
To our knowledge, these two exceptions have not been a barrier to accelerating the adoption and use of our models within our armed forces to date.

The Department of War has stated they will only contract with AI companies who accede to “any lawful use” and remove safeguards in the cases mentioned above. They have threatened to remove us from their systems if we maintain these safeguards; they have also threatened to designate us a “supply chain risk”—a label reserved for US adversaries, never before applied to an American company—and to invoke the Defense Production Act to force the safeguards’ removal. These latter two threats are inherently contradictory: one labels us a security risk; the other labels Claude as essential to national security.

Regardless, these threats do not change our position: we cannot in good conscience accede to their request.'

One piece of good news I haven’t shared (oversight): the whole “supply chain risk” thing needn’t be extremely broad. DoW has a lot of latitude to scope it as narrowly or as broadly as they want. Scoping it narrowly would mean they could keep Claude out of the systems that concern them without doing damage to Anthropic’s broader business.

I really hope DoW considers something like this. The broad scoping would do the following things:

1\. Force all DoW contractors (a large fraction of publicly traded U.S. companies as well as many private firms) to cut ties with Anthropic.
2\. (1) probably means that Anthropic’s ability to raise capital in the future would be in serious jeopardy. Thus they could not continue scaling, and they might be out of the AI competition in surprisingly short order. This also means America would be denied the investment of that capital into the AI and energy infrastructure it would otherwise build.
3\. Stepping back, the whole affair would raise the regulatory risk premium of the entire field of AI. This increases the cost of capital for the entire industry, meaning lower investment and slower development on the margin.
4\. The willingness of USG to exert this kind of control over businesses probably makes a whole host of companies less likely to want to do business with USG going forward.
5\. The same willingness of USG to exert control only exacerbates the fears in other countries about adopting American AI.
6\. DoW being so escalatory here will make Anthropic a martyr, the exact thing the Trump Administration wants to avoid.
7\. The escalation will also make doing business with USG, and especially the military, less popular in the frontier labs. It is possible that signing any new contracts with the military will come to be viewed by the top researchers as an unacceptable moral and ethical compromise. If Anthropic had buckled to DoW demands, they’d face a talent exodus. Right now that exodus would be mostly to other U.S.-based labs, but that isn’t a guarantee. These people are highly mobile, and they can leave the country. A talent exodus from America is not impossible.

Some of this damage has simply been done already. But most of it has not yet. A narrow version of the supply chain risk designation, plus cancellation of Anthropic’s contract, satisfies DoW’s stated objections with Anthropic without so much collateral damage.

🧠 Why Ling-2.5 suggests that Attention at scale should be hybrid?
[@AntLingAGI](https://nitter.net/AntLingAGI "Ant Ling") just unveiled Ling-2.5-1T, its new 1T-parameter flagship. Behind it is a clear architectural thesis: maximize efficiency leverage with less compute.
Zhihu contributor & Ant researcher 知之不止知 breaks it down in "Ling 2.5: Lightning Attention + MLA Hybrid Linear Architecture in Practice"

⚙️ Core idea: Efficiency Leverage > Raw Scale
Ling's base-model design goal: Use lower FLOPs / GPU hours to unlock larger architectural efficiency gains.
From Ling 2.0's Scaling Law analysis, Ant found:
• High-sparsity MoE
• Finer-grained expert routing
→ both significantly improve efficiency.
But there's a catch — The Long-Context Bottleneck🤔
At 4K–8K context, MoE dominates compute.
At 32K+, compute shifts to Attention.
At 256K+, Full Attention (GQA) becomes the absolute bottleneck.
Meanwhile, we're entering the Agent era:
• Deep reasoning
• Multi-step tool calls
• Long-horizon execution
All explode prefill + decode context length.
👉 So, to enable real long-range agents, Attention itself must change.

🧭 Two paths for Attention
1️⃣ Sparse Attention
• Safer, same Full Attention complexity
• Lower theoretical ceiling
2️⃣ Linear Attention
• Changes computation fundamentally
• Much higher upside
Ant pursued both, but focused on linearization for its long-term potential.

🔬 What Ling 2.x research confirmed
From Ling 2.0 → Ling 2.5, Ant validated:
• ❌ Pure Linear Attention has expressivity limits
• ✅ Hybrid Linear + Full Attention can outscale Full Attention at normal windows
• ❌ Hybrid Linear has no inherent long-context or multi-step reasoning weakness
• ✅ Post-training works as well as (or better than) Full Attention
• ✅ Architecture + continued training enables near-zero loss migration
And this became the foundation of Ling 2.5.

🧩 Ling 2.5's key architectural upgrade
Replace Ling 2.0's GQA with MLA + Lightning Attention (1:7 hybrid):
• Lightning Attention → faster kernels, higher long-context throughput
• MLA → extreme KV cache compression
Result: much faster + cheaper long-context inference

🛠️ Solving real incompatibilities (the hard part)
Two major blockers:
1️⃣ QK Norm vs MLA
• QK Norm breaks MLA's KV absorption
• Solution: mathematically fold QK Norm into q\_proj / k\_proj via RMSNorm calibration
2️⃣ Full RoPE vs Partial RoPE
• Ling uses Partial RoPE
• Solution: decouple RoPE-affected dimensions, transform only what's needed, then re-merge → positional correctness preserved

🔄 Smooth migration training (no performance cliff)
Starting from Ling-2.0-1T @ 20T tokens, Ant used staged warmups:
a. GQA → Lightning Attention + GQA
b. Linear warmup (minimal params unfrozen)
c. GQA → MLA (with QK Norm removal)
d. MLA warmup
e. Full-parameter continued training
Result: loss returns to pre-migration level at very low cost.

📊 Why 1:7 is the sweet spot
Scaling Law experiments show (fixed FLOPs):
• 🏆 1:7 → best performance/efficiency balance
• 1:2 / 1:4 → similar quality, much higher inference cost
• 1:16 → cheap inference, but clear loss degradation
Thus Ling 2.5 locks in 1:7.

📚 Data strategy matters too
During 9T continued pretraining:
• Conservative: old data first, then new
• Aggressive: switch to high-quality new data early
✅ And aggressive wins with faster recovery & higher final ceiling — Ling-2.5-1T switches to new data at full-parameter training stage.

🏁 Conclusion
Ling-2.5 is a pragmatic "cost-down, efficiency-up" upgrade.
At 256K+ context, just 10 MLA layers already consume >60% of compute — and Ant is already exploring the next architectural break to push beyond Full Attention limits.

It's telling that Block's share price is back to 2019 levels after laying off 4000 people - but today with 50% more headcount than then.

SWE dev velocity used to be a corporate bottleneck - more hands write code faster so you can ship features on time. LLMs remove that bottleneck. What are the limiting factors now?

\- Product ideation - can you come up with good product ideas consistently? Just because you can write infinite slop, doesn't mean you should ship it
\- Experimentation - if you're shipping more ideas, you need to effectively A/B test and understand which ones are doing well
\- Legal/regulatory/compliance review - 'moving fast' is particularly frowned upon by fintech regulators
\- TAM - if your customers can't spend, building more features can't unlock more value

Jack is right that smaller teams leveraging AI can match the velocity of larger teams. But large teams with AI should be able to expand \*\*even more\*\*. Drastic staff cuts (or CRM's $50B buybacks) is an admission that you're bottlenecked somewhere else now, and you don't know how to fix it. Highly ambitious companies with lots of surface area to tread will continue to find ways to utilize their employees.

Why, as a a lover of AI for legal, I hate AI for longform blog posts:

There is too much content to consume. You won't have time to consume 0.001% of it before you die.

Longform writing is a form of "proof of work", like in Bitcoin. Writing proves two things:
1) That the topic is important enough to dedicate hours of your life to
2) That you deeply thought about the topic

It forces the author to put skin in the game. It's a bet that their time spent will pay off.

In a world of AI writing, we just get inundated. All writers know they could write a persuasive article on any stance, whether it's true or not, whether it's important or not.

Without a new "proof of work" mechanism, we will be flooded with too many ideas too persuasively and won't be able to figure out what matters.

You see this in startups too: Now anyone can write a convincing proposal in seconds. If you ask Claude to create a proposal to add food delivery to your AI app, it will do a great job of creating a convincing doc for why the company should do that.

But without a human having "skin in the game" of proposing the idea, it dramatically devalues the proposal.

Skin in the game is incredibly important.

GPs need skin in the game when they start a fund.

CEOs need skin in the game when they start a company.

Writers need skin in the game (time committed) when they put writing out into the world, otherwise it's likely they are just creating noise to get attention.
