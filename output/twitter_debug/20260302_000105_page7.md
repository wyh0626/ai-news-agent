My thoughts on OpenAI's agreement with the DoD:

On autonomous AI weapons:

1\. "The AI System will not be used to independently direct autonomous weapons in any case where law, regulation, or Department policy requires human control."

This says that OpenAI's models may not direct autonomous weapons where prohibited by law (i.e., where direction of such weapons by a human is required).

But is this, in fact, prohibited by law? OpenAI says that, based on existing laws, regulation and policy, "we are confident that \[DoD's use of OpenAI models to power autonomous weapons\] cannot happen".

2\. "nor will it be used to assume other high-stakes decisions that require approval by a human decisionmaker under the same authorities."

Same point as above. OpenAI clarifies that this provision relates to "systems such as 'social credit'".

3\. "Per DoD Directive 3000.09 (dtd 25 January 2023), any use of AI in autonomous and semi-autonomous systems must undergo rigorous verification, validation, and testing to ensure they perform as intended in realistic environments before deployment."

This sentence does not modify the previous sentence. It's a "belt and suspenders" provision, which says that, to the extent use of AI in autonomous and semi-autonomous systems IS okay under existing law, such systems must go through verification, validation and testing.

On domestic mass surveillance:

4\. "For intelligence activities, any handling of private information will comply with the Fourth Amendment, the National Security Act of 1947 and the Foreign Intelligence and Surveillance Act of 1978, Executive Order 12333, and applicable DoD directives requiring a defined foreign intelligence purpose."

This just says that any handling of private information will comply with law.

5\. "The AI System shall not be used for unconstrained monitoring of U.S. persons’ private information as consistent with these authorities."

This sentence is extremely interesting, since it appears to include a \*flat prohibition" on using AI "for unconstrained monitoring of U.S. persons' private information" - i.e., it concedes that this position is "consistent with \[the Fourth Amendment, etc.\]".

Note the contrast with the next sentence: "The system shall also not be used for domestic law-enforcement activities EXCEPT AS PERMITTED BY the Posse Comitatus Act and other applicable law."

Again, OpenAI says that the contract will not enable the DoD to use OpenAI models to conduct mass surveillance of U.S. persons, based on "existing laws that heavily restrict DoW from domestic surveillance".

Two more points:

a. What happens if the DoD violates the contract by doing things like using OpenAI's models to conduct domestic mass surveillance. The answer: OpenAI would terminate it. "As with any contract, we could terminate it if the counterparty violates the terms."

b. If existing law were to change, OpenAI's stance is that the contract \*would be unaffected\*. "Our contract explicitly references the surveillance and autonomous weapons and policies AS THEY EXIST TODAY, so that even if those laws or policies change in the future, use of our systems must still remain aligned with the current standards reflected in the agreement."

As noted above, this seems correct regarding domestic mass surveillance - see point #5. However, I'm not understanding how the language released by OpenAI provides the same regarding autonomous weapons. It's possible that there is a separate provision regarding this elsewhere in the contract; it would be great if OpenAI could clarify.

Overall: This language is exactly what I expected (and actually is even \*better\* than I expected vis-a-vis domestic mass surveillance).

On mass surveillance:
\- "unconstrained monitoring of U.S. persons' private information" -> so constrained monitoring is fine? what is constrained? is monitoring all of California constrained?
\- there is also no explicit restriction on mass surveillance of non-U.S. persons

On fully autonomous weapons:
\- it says the system won't "independently direct autonomous weapons in any case where law, regulation, or Department policy requires human control" -> if a future (or current) policy interpretation says a particular mode is acceptable with "human-on-the-loop" this contract language may not block it
\- "cloud-only means no edge, therefore no autonomous lethal weapons" -> this is a ridiculous statement, cloud only is no protection against fully autonomous weapons

They also cite DoD 3000.09:
DoD 3000.09 says it does not apply to "autonomous or semi-autonomous cyberspace capabilities"

Furthermore, the contract says the DoW can use the AI system "for all lawful purposes, consistent with applicable law". OpenAI presents this as a safeguard.

But here's the problem: if something is already illegal, the contract isn't adding any protection. The government already can't do illegal things. So the contract is essentially saying "you can do anything you're already allowed to do", which is no constraint at all.
